FAQ

Likely Q&A

Whoever is most comfortable with a question should take it — these answers are here so everyone feels prepared.

Q
Your model only explains 13% of the variance — isn't that bad?
▼

The 13.75% is the in-sample R² for Random Forest on ISE daily returns (the dependent variable). R² does not measure variance in the independent variables.

For daily financial returns, 4–14% is the EMH baseline. Fama (1970) predicts this — markets are informationally efficient so returns are near-random. Walk-forward R² on crisis windows: OLS reaches 74.2% during COVID and 38.6% during GFC. Those are the operative numbers.

Q
Why does the 2021–24 R² go negative?
▼

Negative R² means the model's predictions are worse than simply predicting the mean every time. This signals a structural break — the statistical relationship between global market indices and ISE returns broke down during 2021–24.

Turkey experienced repeated domestic shocks (CBRT firings, unorthodox rate policy, currency collapse) that severed the global co-movement link. Negative R² is the finding, not the problem.

Q
Why did you use yfinance for Model B when the assignment gave you a CSV?
▼

The CSV covers January 2009–August 2011 — the GFC recovery window. It contains 7 global indices. Model B's purpose is to test whether Turkey-specific context (USDTRY velocity, VIX lags, CBRT dummy) improves crisis detection across a 20-year horizon.

None of those features exist in the CSV. Model A strictly uses the provided CSV. Model B is our extension — separate data, separate purpose.

Q
What is the fragility score exactly? How is it calculated?
▼

It is a composite signal derived from three components: (1) prediction error from the walk-forward models — how far off the model is from actual ISE returns; (2) rolling return volatility — 30-day standard deviation of ISE monthly returns; (3) eigenvalue ratio from PCA on the feature correlation matrix — captures systemic co-movement across markets.

These are standardised and combined, then normalised to 0–100. Thresholds at 40 and 70 map to Minsky's Hedge, Speculative, and Ponzi regimes.

Q
Why only four models in the dashboard? You mentioned six.
▼

Ridge converged to OLS — the optimal α was 0.0001, the minimum of our search grid, meaning L2 penalisation provided no benefit. Ridge is subsumed into the OLS result.

ANN's cross-validation RMSE was 0.0369 — six times its test RMSE of 0.006 — a classic overfitting signature on a 536-row sample. Zhang (2003) documents this failure mode explicitly. Both are fully reported in Section 2 notebooks. The four displayed models provide distinct signal; the excluded two are degenerate cases.

Q
How does Model A score months it wasn't trained on (e.g. 2018, 2021)?
▼

Model A is trained on the 2009–2011 CSV. The fragility scoring pipeline takes that trained model and runs it forward on monthly yfinance data from 2003 to 2026 — the model produces predictions on out-of-sample data, and those prediction errors feed into the fragility composite.

So Model A's 33.5 score in November 2021 is a genuine out-of-sample prediction — the model hasn't seen that data, which is why its error is large and the R² is negative.

Q
Why is Model B's walk-forward R² also negative in 2021–24?
▼

Even with Turkey-specific features, 2021–24 breaks both models at the walk-forward prediction level. The fragility score for Model B is elevated (53.0 in Nov 2021) because the score composite includes rolling volatility and TRY velocity — signals that don't require accurate return prediction.

The walk-forward R² measures prediction accuracy; the fragility score measures systemic stress. They are different things. Model B's value is that it keeps the score elevated even when prediction breaks down.

Q
Why Minsky? Is this standard in financial ML?
▼

Minsky's Financial Instability Hypothesis provides the theoretical framework for why stability leads to fragility. The Hedge → Speculative → Ponzi transition maps directly to increasing leverage and vulnerability in financial markets.

It's been applied to emerging market crises extensively (Kindleberger, 2000; Borio, 2014). Using it as a regime labelling framework gives the composite score interpretability that a raw prediction error wouldn't have.

Q
What are the limitations of your approach?
▼

Four main limitations:

(1) The assignment CSV is only 32 months — too narrow to train a robust time-series model, which is why Model B was necessary.

(2) The fragility score is a composite index, not a probability — it cannot be directly backtested as a trading signal without a defined decision rule.

(3) ANN underperformed due to sample size, limiting the deep learning component.

(4) The ISE training data contained a circuit-breaker artifact — on days when trading was halted, the original cleaned CSV forward-filled the last valid price, creating ~68 rows with artificially repeated values. This was identified, corrected, and the models were re-run against the fixed data. The key numbers were unchanged (Nov 2021 gap: 33.5 vs 53.0; Mar 2020: 58.8 vs 71.1; walk-forward R² identical) confirming the artifact had negligible impact on findings.

Q
What is SHAP and why did you use it?
▼

SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) assigns each feature a marginal contribution to the model's output, based on game theory. It provides global feature importance (averaged across all observations) and local explanations (for individual predictions).

The key finding: Model A's top features are all global indices. Model B's top features include VIX lag and USDTRY volatility — confirming the extended model captures information the baseline cannot access. This directly addresses the professor's emphasis on interpretable AI.

Q
Did you test for data leakage?
▼

Yes — walk-forward validation is specifically designed to prevent look-ahead bias. Each split trains strictly on data before the crisis window and tests on the crisis window itself. There is no overlap between training and test periods. This is the standard approach for time-series cross-validation in financial forecasting.

Q
Why not use LSTM or a proper deep learning approach?
▼

LSTM requires substantially more data than 536 rows to train meaningfully. On this sample size, gradient-based sequence models overfit severely — our ANN result (CV RMSE 6× test RMSE) illustrates this.

The literature (Zhang, 2003; Makridakis et al., 2018) consistently shows that simple, well-regularised models outperform deep learning on small financial time series. Our choice of Random Forest as the primary non-linear model is deliberate.
